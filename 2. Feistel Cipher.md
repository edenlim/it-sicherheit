# Important Foundation
**Plaintext**: Your original data, before encryption

**Ciphertext**: Your data, after encryption

**Block Cipher**: A Block Cipher is a type of symmetric encryption that processes data in fixed-size chunks called Blocks. Block size is usually `128 bits == 16 Bytes` 
- **Smaller**: If the plaintext is shorter than a full block, you must add extra bits called **Padding** to fill it up.
- **Larger**: If your plaintext is larger than a block, you use Operating Modes (like ECB or CBC) to decide how the blocks are linked together during encryption

**XOR**: Exclusive OR. XOR is its own inverse. Feistel Cipher uses this magic property to encrypt and decrypt data.
- Example with 1 and 0
    |$\oplus$|1|0|
    |---|---|---|
    |1|F|T|
    |0|T|F|
    - Notice that (1,0) and (0,1) are the same
- Example with Plaintext $P$, Ciphertext $C$ and Key $K$
    - $P \oplus K = C$ 
    - $C \oplus K = P$ 

# XOR Cipher
XOR Cipher is used in symmetric encryption/decrption. The same key can be used to encrypt and decrypt a message.

## How it works
Plaintexts, Ciphertext and Keys are stored always in a long list of 1s and 0s. They all have to be the same **length**.

In this example:
- $P$ is `9`: bit value of `1001` 
- $K$ is `6`: bit value of `0101`
- $C$ is `C`: bit value of `1100`

### Encryption
When encrypting a Plaintext, you perform:
- $P \oplus K = C$ 

    The cipher process will compare each element in $P$ and $K$ at the same position and perform XOR on the elements. The process is defined below:

    **In Code terms**
    ```js
    for(int i = 0; i < length; i++){
        cipher[i] = plaintext[i] ^ key[i] // ^ is XOR operator
    }
    ```
    **In Math terms**
    $$
    \begin{array}{cccccc} & 1 & 0 & 0 & 1 & & \text{(P)} \\ \oplus & 0 & 1 & 0 & 1 & & \text{(K)} \\ \hline & 1 & 1 & 0 & 0 & & \text{(C)} \end{array}
    $$

    - The End result is Ciphertext $C$, which has the value of `C`: bit value of `1100`

### Decryption
When decrypting the Ciphertext, you use the same key to decrpyt, since XOR is its own inverse
- $C \oplus K = P$ 
    
    **In Code terms**
    ```js
    for(int i = 0; i < length; i++){
        plaintext[i] = cipher[i] ^ key[i] // ^ is XOR operator
    }
    ```
    **In Math terms**
    $$
        \begin{array}{cccccc} 
            & 1 & 1 & 0 & 0 & & \text{(C)} 
            \\ 
            \oplus & 0 & 1 & 0 & 1 & & \text{(K)} 
            \\ 
            \hline 
            & 1 & 0 & 0 & 1 & & \text{(P)} 
        \end{array}
    $$

    - The End result is Plaintext $P$, which has the value of `9`: bit value of `1001`

# Feistel Cipher
Feistel Cipher is a more complicated Cipher that utilizes XOR Ciphering.
## Requirements
Feistel Cipher requires 3 things to start:
1. The input Block **in bits**, that can be divisible by 2.
2. Round function $F$
    - A set of mathematical operations that are repeated multiple times to transform the input into something that looks completely random. 
    - Can be as complex or simple as desired
    - It is a random PRF function (Pseudo Random Function: If given the same variable, the function always return the same values)
3. A Main key $K$. The Key is a string of binary bit.

## Expansion
Feistel Cipher will use the three things to create the following:
1. $Inputs\ L$ and $R$: The input block will be split into 2 equal halves: Left and Right.
2. Subkey $K_n$: Subkeys are created from the main key
    - Each Subkey used only **ONCE**. 
    - Subkeys **MUST** be as long as $L$ and $R$. 
    - Subkeys are **Slices of key**, exploded() into smaller strings.
        - These subkey strings can be Transformed (Rotated, Shifted, XORed)
3. Roundkey: Round keys are keys generated from $F(Input,Subkey)$

## Begin with Encryption
After splitting the input blocks in halve and generating the subkeys, we begin with Feistel Cipher, which has **3 Steps**:

Being: ($L_1$, $R_1)

1. Current Value: ($L_1$, $R_1$)
    1. **Get your RoundKey**: Obtain Roundkey from round function F using the second input parameter and the subkey: $F(R_1, K_1)$.
    2. **Encrypting $L_1$**: Perform XOR Cipher on first parameter $L_1$ with Roundkey $F(R_1, K_1)$ to get Encrypted $L_1 \oplus F(R_1, K_1)$

2. Current Value: ($L_1 \oplus F(R_1, K_1)$, $R_1$)
    1. We swap $L_1 \oplus F(R_1, K_1)$ with $R_1$

3. Current Value: ($R_1$, $L_1 \oplus F(R_1, K_1)$)
      1. **Get your RoundKey**: Obtain Roundkey from round function F using the second input parameter and the subkey: $F(L_1 \oplus F(R_1, K_1), K_2)$.
    2. **Encrypting $R_1$**: Perform XOR Cipher on the first parameter $R_1$ with Roundkey $F(L_1 \oplus F(R_1, K_1), K_2)$ to get Encrypted $R_1 \oplus F(L_1 \oplus F(R_1, K_1), K_2)$


The final result is ($R_1 \oplus F(L_1 \oplus F(R_1, K_1), K_2)$, $L_1 \oplus F(R_1, K_1)$)

### End result
We then typically concencate our two parameters into one block:

(($R_1 \oplus F(L_1 \oplus F(R_1, K_1), K_2)$) + ($L_1 \oplus F(R_1, K_1)$))

### Example
**Starting Variables**
- Plaintext: `B6` = `10110110`
    - $L$: `B` = `1011`
    - $R$: `6` = `0110`

- Key
    - $K_1$: `3` = `0011`
    - $K_2$: `C` = `1100`

- Round Function
    - $F(X,K) = X\ \text{AND}\ K$


**Start of Encryption**
|Step|Status|What is done here|Calculation|
|---|---|---|:---:|
|1|feistel(`B`,`6`)|Input L and R into the Feistel Function|-|
|2|Get Roundkey 1|Roundkey = `2` = F(`6`,`3`) |$\begin{array}{rcccc} & 0 & 1 & 1 & 0 \\ \text{AND} & 0 & 0 & 1 & 1 \\ \hline 2 = & 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 \end{array}$|
|3|Encrypt `B`|Encrypt = `B` $\oplus$ `2` to get L´<br>L´ = `9`|$\begin{array}{rcccc} & 1 & 0 & 1 & 1 \\ \oplus & 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 \\ \hline 9 = & 1 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{array}$|
|4|Swap(`9`,`6`)|With L Encrypted, we swap with right and begin again|New pair: (`6`,`9`)|
|5|feistel(`6`,`9`)|Input R and L´ into the Feistel Function|-|
|6|Get Roundkey 2|Roundkey = `8` = F(`9`,`C`)|$\begin{array}{rcccc} & 1 & 0 & 0 & 1\\ \text{AND} & 1 & 1 & 0 & 0 \\ \hline 8 = & 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{array}$|
|7|Encrpyt `6`|Encrypt `6` $\oplus$ `8` to get R´<br>R´ = `E`|$\begin{array}{rcccc} & 0 & 1 & 1 & 0 \\ \oplus & 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ \hline E = & 1 & 1 & 1 & 0\end{array}$|
|8|Concat(R´,L´)|`E9`|-|

**Result**: Ciphertext `E9`

## Begin with Decryption
We repeat the process in backwards. Even the subkey must be in backwards

### Example
**Starting Variables**
- Ciphertext: `E9` = `11101001`
    - $L$: `E` = `1110`
    - $R$: `9` = `1001`

- Key
    - $K_2$: `C` = `1100`
    - $K_1$: `3` = `0011`

- Round Function
    - $F(X,K) = X\ \text{AND}\ K$


**Start of Encryption**
|Step|Status|What is done here|Calculation|
|---|---|---|:---:|
|1|feistel(`E`,`9`)|Input L and R into the Feistel Function|-|
|2|Get Roundkey 2|Roundkey = `8` = F(`9`,`C`) |$\begin{array}{rcccc} & 1 & 0 & 0 & 1 \\ \text{AND} & 1 & 1 & 0 & 0 \\ \hline 8 = & 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{array}$|
|3|Decrypt `E`|Encrypt = `E` $\oplus$ `8` to get L´<br>L´ = `6`|$\begin{array}{rcccc} & 1 & 1 & 1 & 0 \\ \oplus & 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ \hline 6 = & 0 & 1 & 1 & 0 \end{array}$|
|4|Swap(`6`,`9`)|With L Decrypted, we swap with right and begin again|New pair: (`9`,`6`)|
|5|feistel(`9`,`6`)|Input R and L´ into the Feistel Function|-|
|6|Get Roundkey 1|Roundkey = `8` = F(`6`,`3`)|$\begin{array}{rcccc} & 0 & 1 & 1 & 0\\ \text{AND} & 0 & 0 & 1 & 1 \\ \hline 2 = & 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 \end{array}$|
|7|Decrypt `9`|Decrypt `9` $\oplus$ `2` to get R´<br>R´ = `B`|$\begin{array}{rcccc} & 1 & 0 & 0 & 1 \\ \oplus & 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 \\ \hline B = & 1 & 0 & 1 & 1\end{array}$|
|8|Concat(R´,L´)|`B6`|-|

**Result**: Plaintext `B6`
