# Difference between Asymmetric and Symmetric Cryptography
**Vor- und Nachteile**

- **Symmetrischer**: 
    - **Advantage**
        - **Efficiency**: Symmetrischer Verschlüsselung ist sehr gut für die schnelle Verschlüsselung großer Datenmengen geeignet.
        - **Ease of Implementation**: Eine effiziente Implementierung ist mit nur wenigen Zeilen Code möglich.
    - **Disadvantage**
        - **Sharing of Key**: Die größte Herausforderung besteht darin, den geheimen Schlüssel sicher von person $A$ zu person $B$ zu übertragen.
        - **Key Management**: Bei $n$ Nutzern werden $\frac{n \cdot (n-1)}{2}$ Schlüssel benötigt. Die Anzahl der Schlüssel wächst quadratisch, abhängig von der Anzahl der Nutzer.
        - **No non-repudiation**: Es gibt weder einen Herkunftsnachweis noch eine digitale Signatur, da jede Person ver- oder entschlüsseln kann; somit mangelt es an der Zurechenbarkeit.
- **Asymmetrischer**:
    - **Advantage**
        - **Key Establishment**: Ermöglicht die Verteilung von Schlüsseln über unsichere Kanäle.
        - **Non-repudiation**: Ermöglicht digitale Signaturen, wodurch eindeutig wird, wer ein Dokument verschlüsselt oder unterschrieben hat.
        - **Authenticity**: Kann durch Zertifikate genutzt werden, um die Identität des Kommunikationspartners zweifelsfrei festzustellen.
    - **Disadvantage**
        - **Performance**: Lange Schlüssel führen zu einer langsamen Verschlüsselung; das Verfahren ist daher ungeeignet für die schnelle Verschlüsselung großer Datenmengen.
        - **Key Length**: Asymmetrische Kryptosysteme erfordern signifikant höhere Bit-Längen, um ein zu symmetrischen Verfahren äquivalentes Sicherheitslevel zu erreichen.
        - **Vulnerability**: Ohne Authentifizierung der öffentlichen Schlüssel durch Zertifikate ist das Verfahren anfällig für Man-In-The-Middle (MITM) Angriffe.

# How it works
In Asymmetric Function, everyone has two keys:
- **Public Key**: A key that is opened to the public, and your public key will be used in the encryption function when specifically sent to you
- **Private Key**: A key that is kept secret. This key is used to decrypt any incoming message

$$
x = \text{Cleartext}\\
y = \text{Ciphertext}\\
e(x) = \text{Encryption function}\\
d(y) = \text{Decryption function}\\
k_{pub} = \text{Public key}\\
k_{pr} = \text{Private key}\\
n = \text{RSA Modulus}\\
e = \text{Public Exponent}\\
d = \text{Private Exponent}\\
\phi(n) = \text{Euler's Totient Function} = (p-1)•(q-1)\\
n = (p•q)
p,q = \text{Two random and large Prime Number}
$$

- Coprime: If the only common divisor between two numbers are 1, they are coprime of each other.
    - Example: 10 and 9
        - $10 = \{1,2,5,10\}$
        - $9 = \{1,3,9\}$
- $\phi(n)$: Counting from 1 to $n - 1$, the amount of numbers that are coprime to $n$
    - $\phi(10) = \{1,3,7,9\} = 4$ 
- $p, q$: They are two randomly generated **large** prime numbers

## RSA Algorithm
- $A(x)$ = The representation of the bits in polynomial form
- $P(x)$ = The irreducible Polynomial equation given
    - A polynomial that cannot be factored into the product of two non-constant polynomials
$$
\begin{array}{}
       & a & b & q & s & t\\
    1. & P(x) & A(x) & q_1 & s_1 & A^{-1}(x)\\
    2. & A(x) & \frac{P(x)}{A(x)} & q_2 & s_2 & t_2\\
    ...\\
    n-1. & b_{n-2} & \frac{a_{n-2}}{b_{n-2}} & q_{n-1} & 1 & 0 \\
    n. & b_{n-1} & 1 & - & 1 & 0
\end{array}
$$

### Abwartsschritt
- $a´ = b$
- $b´ = \frac{a}{b}$
- $q = \text{The value of } (x^y) \text{ that } b \text{ has to multiply to have the same highest Potenz as } a$
### Aufwartsschritt
- $s´ = t_{below}$
- $t´ = s_{below} \oplus (q \cdot t_{below})$

## Diffie-Hellman and Elgamal
In a Diffie-Hellman encryption:

**Steps**
1. two party Alice $A$ and Bob $B$ generate their public keys independently
2. they exchange their public key with each other
3. Calculate their shared secret key that they will sue for encryption

1. Diffie-Hellman (The Setup)
    - Function: Key Agreement / Key Exchange.
    - How it works: Alice and Bob mathematically combine their private keys with each other's public keys.
    - The Result: Both arrive at the exact same Shared Secret Key ($K$).
    - Limitation: No actual message (like "Hello" or a file) is transmitted during this process. They only agree on the password.

2. Elgamal (The Execution)
    - Function: Asymmetric Encryption and Decryption.
    - How it works: It requires a Diffie-Hellman exchange to happen first.
        1. Alice and Bob use DH to generate the shared secret $K$.
        2. Encryption: Alice takes her actual message ($M$) and multiplies it by the shared secret $K$ modulo $p$. This creates the ciphertext ($C$).
        3. Decryption: Bob receives $C$ and divides it by the shared secret $K$ (using the modular inverse) to reveal the original message $M$.
    - The Result: A secure transfer of data over the internet.

### Formulars
1. **Public Key calculation**
$$
p = \text{Public Prime}\\
g = \text{generator}\\
\text{Public Key } k_{public} = g^{k_{secret}} (\text{mod } p)
$$


2. **Secret Key calculation**
$$
X_{key} =  \text{Received Public Key from the other corresponder}\\
k_{secret} = \text{Your own secret key}\\
p = \text{Public Prime}\\

\text{Shared Secret Key} K = X_{key}^{k_{secret}} (\text{mod } p)
$$

# Others
A. Man-In-The-Middle (MITM) Attack
- Definition: An attacker intercepts the communication channel between Alice and Bob. 
- Vulnerability: It works against all public-key systems. Alice believes she is using Bob's public key, but she is actually using the attacker's public key (and vice versa).  
- Solution: MITM attacks are prevented by using Certificates.  


B. Digital Signatures
- Purpose: Analogous to a real-life signature, but practically impossible to forge. It ensures Data Integrity, Authenticity, and Non-repudiation (Nichtabstreitbarkeit).  
- Mechanism: It combines a one-way hash function with asymmetric cryptography.  
- The Step-by-Step Process:
    1. The file to be signed is hashed. 
    2. The hash is encrypted using the sender's private key (this is the opposite of normal encryption, which uses the public key). 
    3. This encrypted hash (the signature) is attached to the file. 
    4. The receiver can use the sender's public key to decrypt the hash and verify the signature.  


C. Certificates
- Definition: A digital document that provides proof of authenticity for a public key. It cryptographically binds an identity to a public key.  
- Certificate Authority (CA): Certificates are signed by a trusted third-party organization called a CA. Operating systems and browsers come pre-loaded with CA "Root Certificates".  
- Chain of Trust: Certificates form a hierarchy. Every certificate is signed by the private key of the certificate above it, all the way up to the Root CA. If a Root CA becomes invalid, all child certificates lose trust. 
-  Revocation: Certificates are only valid for a specific timeframe and can be revoked early by the CA using a Certificate Revocation List (CRL) if they are compromised

# 1. The Core Vulnerability: Lack of Identity

The fundamental flaw in standard public-key exchanges (like basic Diffie-Hellman) is that public keys are just raw numbers.  

They do **not** contain a name tag or proof of ownership.

When Alice receives a public key:
- The mathematics work perfectly.
- But the math **cannot verify who actually sent the key**.

---

# 2. The Attack Mechanics (The Math)

Here is the exact mathematical breakdown of why the Man-In-The-Middle (MITM) attack succeeds when keys are unverified.

## Alice's Perspective
She receives Oscar's fake key \( Z \).

She calculates the shared secret:
\[
K_A \equiv Z^a \pmod p
\]

---

## Bob's Perspective
He receives Oscar's fake key \( Z \).

He calculates the shared secret:
\[
K_B \equiv Z^b \pmod p
\]

---

## Oscar's Perspective

Oscar generated the fake key \( Z \) using his own private key \( z \), so he can compute both shared secrets:

- Using Alice's intercepted key \( A \):
\[
K_A \equiv A^z \pmod p
\]

- Using Bob's intercepted key \( B \):
\[
K_B \equiv B^z \pmod p
\]

---

## Result

Oscar now has **two separate secure tunnels**:
- One with Alice
- One with Bob

He can:
1. Decrypt Alice's data using \( K_A \)
2. Read or modify it
3. Re-encrypt it using \( K_B \) for Bob

Neither Alice nor Bob realizes they are not talking directly to each other.

---

# 3. The Solution: Certificates

## What is a Certificate?

A certificate **cryptographically binds**:
- A Public Key (\( K_{pub} \))
- To an Identity (\( ID \))

---

## Structure

A certificate consists of:
- Public Key
- Identity
- CA's Digital Signature

---

## How It Prevents the Attack

If Oscar intercepts the bank’s certificate, he tries to replace the public key with his fake key \( Z \).

However:

- The entire certificate is protected by the **CA's Digital Signature**
- If Oscar modifies even **one bit**, the signature becomes invalid
- Oscar **cannot forge a new signature** because he does not have the CA’s private key

---

## Verification Process

Alice’s browser:
1. Checks the certificate’s signature
2. Uses the trusted Root CA list (stored in the OS/browser)
3. Verifies authenticity

If the signature check fails:
- The connection is **rejected immediately**

---

# One-Line Recap

Diffie-Hellman secures communication mathematically, but **certificates are what secure identity**.
